Kenya ranked third most corrupt country in East Africa, South Sudan leads

Posted by Chauncey Koziol on Saturday, February 24, 2024

- According to a Transparency International report, South Sudan is the most corrupt country in East Africa followed by Burundi and then Kenya at position three

- Rwanda was ranked the region's least corrupt country, followed by Tanzania as the second least corrupt East African country

- The corruption levels were expected to increase exponentially during elections in Tanzania, Uganda and Kenya in 2020, 2021 and 2022 respectively

The Corruption Perception Index 2019 report by Transparency International has placed Kenya as the third most corrupt country in East Africa.

According to the report, South Sudan led followed by Burundi and then Kenya in third place, a position it tied with Uganda.

South Sudan managed a paltry 12 points out of the global average of 100. Burundi garnered 19 while Kenya and Uganda both scored 28 points.

The report placed Rwanda as the region's least corrupt country, scoring an impressive 43 points out of 100. Tanzania was ranked the second least corrupt East African country with 37 points.

Countries which perform well on matters fighting corruption have a tendency to enforce strong regulations in managing public finance on top of embracing political consultation between leaders and the populace.

Whereas countries where these regulations perennially score an average of 70 points, those with loopholes in the system average 34 points and below.

The corruption levels were expected to increase exponentially as Tanzania, Uganda and Kenya during general elections in 2020, 2021 and 2022 respectively.

This is due to the fact that politicians start crafting ways to make money for use during the capital-intensive campaigns, the bulk of which comes from the public coffers.

Transparency International maintains that there is need for East African countries to strengthen prosecution laws so that corrupt leaders are brought to justice and convicted.

A 2019 Global Corruption Barometer-Africa by Transparency International shows that more than 50 percent of African citizens are not only worried at the continued rise in cases of graft, but are also disappointed at how their governments are slow with clamping down on the vice.

As this happens, corruption continues to be a huge stumbling block to the continent's economic growth.
