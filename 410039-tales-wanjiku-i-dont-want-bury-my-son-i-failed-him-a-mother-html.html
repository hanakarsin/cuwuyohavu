Tales of Wanjiku: &quot;I Don't Want to Bury My Son, I Failed Him as A Mother&quot;

Posted by Hettie Henneman on Thursday, July 4, 2024

- After being in a troubled marriage for years, Emily left her husband and took her four children along

- The struggle to provide for them, however, saw her spend many hours away from them that she neglected her parenting duties

- She now blames herself for the loss of direction of her firstborn son who is into substance abuse, with fears that one day he may fail to come back home alive

One of the biggest headaches for any parent, especially a mother, is living with everyday fear that it is only a matter of time before the worst happens to her child.

That is the life Emily lives since discovering that her 16-year-old son Declan has got into bad company and may soon face the wrath of the law, or death.

Being a single mother of four, Emily regrets the fact that she spent too much of her time hustling to make ends meet that she missed out on the red flags.

"When COVID-19 hit I was forced to spend more time at home because business was down. That is when I noticed massive changes in Declan," she told Lynn Ngugi.

It was at this point that she started digging to unearth what was happening to her firstborn son, and the findings were heartbreaking.

Emily reveals that after tailgating her son to know where he was hanging out, she watched in shock as her son walked into a den known to harbour young men and women who are into substance abuse.

"I told the seller to stop dishing out illicit substances to my son because he is a minor, but he maintained that he is in business, therefore, cannot stop his customers from buying whatever they want," remembered Emily.

Instead of making things better, the move angered her son who told her to stop sniffing around his life because, according to him, he was no longer a child.

The sad mother traces her son's loss of direction to the day their lifestyle changed after she split up with her ex-husband.

She explains that the family lived in Saika Estate where Declan had a better life including his own room, so moving to Kibera slums angered him so much that he became rebellious.

"I would have loved for my children to continue being raised in a complete family, but my marriage was full of grief and I needed peace of mind. So when I moved out I carried my children along, that's how we ended up here," she expressed.

She is, however, adamant that it is her duty to fend for the rest of her children and give them a better education yet it is difficult to secure a stable job having dropped out of school at Form Three.

These days, Emily makes a living by selling sausages, boiled eggs, and chapati around Community estate, a business that demands her presence from as early as 5am.

"I dropped out of high school and that disappointed my mother. It is a path I would not want my son to follow because he will soon end up suffering like myself," said a teary Emily.

Declan's case has opened Emily's eyes wide enough for her to learn how to balance business and parenting. That is why she never has second thoughts about calling it a day when the time for her children comes.

With every new dawn, the mother wakes to pray for the young man to change his ways while hoping that he will remain alive and free because one day he might not make it back home alive.

"With Declan, all we do is hope that we shall see him. Some days he comes, others he doesn't," she lamented.
